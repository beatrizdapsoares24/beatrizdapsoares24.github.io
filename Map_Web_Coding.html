SDG 6.Ensure access to water and sanitation for all.

SDG Target 6.1 By 2030, achieve universal and equitable access to safe and affordable drinking water for all.

The map displays data from the United Nations on the share of the population with access to safely managed drinking water services by country in 2024. The map correlates to Sustainable Development Goal 6, which mandates "Ensure access to water and sanitation for all," specifically target 6.1, which aims to “By 2030, achieve universal and equitable access to safe and affordable drinking water for all.” The precise indicator used in the model is 6.1.1: “Portion of population using safely managed drinking water services”. It can be seen on the map that various countries are missing data, hence hindering the ability to gain a well-rounded perspective on patterns of water availability around the world. Nonetheless, the countries with the lowest percentages (less than 30%) have been labelled on the map to highlight where water insecurity is most prominent. The lowest of the countries with available data are Tuvalu (10.37%), the Central African Republic (11.25%), and Sierra Leone (12.49%). Except for Australia, Greenland and a handful of European nations, it appears that a majority of the missing data occurs in developing countries, primarily in Africa and South America.


Chloropleth maps are a mapping approach that utilises colours and shading to represent numerical values and ranges cartographically. Chloropleth maps represent quantitative data rather than qualitative data and are a powerful tool in using visual aids to communicate information at different geographical scales. The use of colour in chloropleth maps can be through gradients (light-to-dark or vice-versa) or through colour differences in hues and saturation, resulting in diverse potential colour schemes. An important element in the creation of chloropleth maps is to take into consideration the significance of colours, since different hues and schemes correlate to different interpretations. For example, a popular colour correlation is green = good, and red = bad, a representation that has been widely used in visual media. Some benefits of chloropleth maps include how easy they are to create, as well as how they are relatively easy to understand, primarily due to people being familiar with the format of chloropleth maps. However, chloropleth maps also have limitations. One of these limitations is the colour symbolization I previously mentioned. Another includes the need for normalising absolute values and the unreliability of estimating values based on colours when reading chloropleth maps. Moreover, many chloropleth maps are problematic for individuals who suffer from colour-blindness since the symbolism and use of differing gradients and colour schemes become less clear depending on the degree and type of colour blindness. To overcome this issue, specific colour palettes have been developed in GIS systems to accommodate individuals with colour-blindness complications.
